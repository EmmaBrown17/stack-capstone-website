Project Overview

This MHCI Capstone project reimagines student exploration by addressing a key challenge: How can we give students agency over their educational path? Through comprehensive stakeholder interviews, extensive prototyping, and iterative testing, our team designed Stack - an intelligent support system that transforms how students, families, and faculty navigate high school.

Students have motivation and support to self-reflect, but they need help getting started and knowing what to reflect on.

Our Research Journey

1

Problem Discovery & Stakeholder Interviews

We began by conducting extensive interviews with students, parents, teachers, and guidance counselors to understand the current challenges in academic exploration and decision-making.

Key Finding: Students want to explore but often don't know where to start or how their choices connect to future opportunities.
2

Parallel Prototyping (12 Prototypes)

We tested diverse concepts including AI recommendation systems, failure reflection cards, skill visualization tools, and gamified exploration platforms to understand what resonated with users.

Key Finding: Students respond positively to gamification and peer connection, but need something that feels different from existing RIASEC assessments.
3

Concept Consolidation & Vision Alignment

Through collaborative exercises and individual visualizations, we converged on five core system components: collaboration, guided freedom, opportunity awareness, embracing uncertainty, and connecting actions to outcomes.

Key Finding: Low-risk environments support exploration - students are more likely to try new things when there's no fear of failure.
4

Iterative Design & User Testing

We developed higher-fidelity prototypes focusing on user flow, visualization methods, reflection processes, and stakeholder communication features.

Key Finding: Information needs concrete context - it's not enough to present opportunities without explaining why they might be relevant.

Key Prototypes & Learnings

Our parallel prototyping phase tested 12 distinct concepts, each addressing different aspects of student exploration and decision-making. Here are some of the most impactful prototypes:

1

AI Recommendation Trust System

Tested whether students trust AI-generated academic recommendations as much as human guidance.

Result: Students rated AI recommendations equally to human ones (3.95 vs 3.8 out of 5), showing willingness to trust intelligent systems.
"The recommendations are dead on" - Parent participant
2

Failure Reflection Cards

Explored whether students can learn growth mindset through peer stories of failure and recovery.

Result: Motivation after setbacks increased from 3.6 to 4.2, with students finding peer stories highly relatable and normalizing.
"I'll feel more confident in myself when taking tests" - Student participant
7

Campaign Craft (RPG Platform)

Gamified skill building through quest-based learning experiences with collaborative elements.

Result: Students were more motivated to join group activities when requests felt specific to their skills, and valued competitive elements.
"If you make it a ranking system or leaderboard, that competitive nature would help increase engagement" - Student participant
4

Ikigai Identity Cards

Used Japanese concept of "reason for being" to help students reflect on identity through four key domains.

Result: Students thought about strengths in new ways and found the process more grounded than traditional career assessments.
"This made me feel like my hobbies are actually skills" - Student participant
9

Imposter Syndrome Workshop

Paired vulnerability exercise where students challenged each other's inner critic through peer validation.

Result: Students felt less alone and more connected, with peer validation proving powerful for disrupting negative self-talk.
"I thought I was the only one who felt that way, but I wasn't" - Student participant
12

Tower Town (Stakeholder Collaboration)

Explored gamified community collaboration between students, parents, teachers, and guidance counselors.

Result: Different stakeholders play distinct roles - parents offer support, teachers provide focus, counselors create actionable plans.
"Student-led and needs-based... hitting that perfect zone where students want to develop more but can't do it without adult guidance" - Teacher participant

Key Insights & Design Implications

🎯 Guided Reflection is Essential

Students value reflection but don't know how to begin. Our system needs to provide prompts and interactive elements that help students clarify their thoughts and initiate meaningful self-discovery.

🎮 Gamification + Social Connection Drive Engagement

Students responded exceptionally well to prototypes with gamified aspects and peer interaction. Vulnerability felt safer when it came from peers, and competition motivated exploration.

🛡️ Low-Risk Environments Support Exploration

Students are more likely to try new things when there's no fear of failure or long-term commitment. Our solution needs to lower barriers to experimentation.

🔄 Beyond RIASEC

Stakeholders want something that feels different from existing personality assessments. Our solution can't just be a reskin - it needs to offer genuinely new value and approaches.

📱 Dynamic & Personalized Systems

The system needs to evolve with students, updating to their changing skills and interests. Static recommendations feel irrelevant; dynamic, personalized guidance builds trust.

🤝 Stakeholder Collaboration

Different stakeholders play unique roles: parents provide support and start conversations, teachers help narrow focus, and counselors create actionable plans. Our system should facilitate these distinct contributions.
